Snow Leopard

FACTS

The snow leopard (*Panthera uncia*), often called the “ghost of the mountains,” is a medium-sized big cat adapted to the cold, rugged terrain of Central and South Asia. It primarily inhabits mountainous regions at elevations of 9,800–17,000 feet, including the Himalayas, the Tibetan Plateau, and the Altai Mountains. Known for their thick, spotted fur and long tails, snow leopards are perfectly suited for camouflage and balance in rocky environments.

FOODS

Snow leopards are carnivores, preying mainly on mountain ungulates such as blue sheep (bharal), ibex, and argali. They also hunt smaller mammals like marmots, hares, and birds. As solitary and stealthy hunters, they can take down prey up to three times their body weight. Their diet varies depending on prey availability and season.

CRISIS

Habitat loss due to livestock grazing, human settlement, and infrastructure development threatens the snow leopard’s survival. They are also at risk from retaliatory killings by herders protecting their livestock and poaching for their pelts and bones, which are used in traditional medicine. Climate change further exacerbates habitat degradation by altering prey distribution and reducing snow cover.

Where to Find

In China, snow leopards are found in the remote, high-altitude regions of the Tibetan Plateau and the Tianshan and Qilian Mountains, spanning the provinces of Xinjiang, Qinghai, Gansu, and Tibet. China hosts the largest population of snow leopards globally, making its conservation efforts critical for the species' survival.

There are an estimated 4,080-6,590 snow leopards in the wild, the natural barren makes it difficult to track them. The snow leopard is listed as Vulnerable on the IUCN Red.
